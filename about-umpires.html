Umpiring in the RDCA

About Umpires

The history, spirit and standards behind umpiring in the Ringwood & District Cricket Association.

History

The RDCA Umpires Association was formed in 1955 under its founding President, Reg Johnston, who recognised the need for a group of independent adjudicators for the Association's higher-grade matches at a time when interest in cricket was growing quickly.

Umpiring has always been seen as a natural next step for players who want to stay involved in the game while putting less strain on ageing bodies. The dedication of the panel is valued by everyone who relies on impartial match management, and members continually work to sharpen their knowledge of the Laws and local Rules, as well as their on-field management skills, so that matches are played free of any undesirable aspects.

The Spirit of Cricket

Cricket owes much of its unique appeal to the principle that it should be played not only within its Laws, but also within the Spirit of the Game. Any action that abuses that spirit causes injury to the game itself, and the major responsibility for upholding fair play rests with the captains.

Respect

The RDCA expects all players and officials to approach every aspect of cricket with respect for the ideals under which the game should be played. The Association's purpose is to play and foster cricket free from any objectionable features, and to build acquaintance and fellowship among players.

The Laws of Cricket devote a specific section to the spirit of the game and the responsibility of captains and players. In keeping with that, all players, officials and spectators involved in RDCA matches are expected to respect the Rules and Laws under which matches operate — knowing them well and applying them without fear or favour.
